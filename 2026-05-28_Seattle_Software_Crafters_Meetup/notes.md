## Full Announcement

AI is transforming software delivery--but without Agile's technical practices, it can create chaos faster than ever. Used well, AI accelerates delivery, improves quality, and frees teams for innovation. Used poorly, it turns small mistakes into expensive disasters at record speed.

In this live, interactive session, David Scott Bernstein--Agile thought leader, AI collaborator, and author--shows how Agile principles and practices form the foundation for AI success.

You'll discover how clarity, iteration, and shared understanding--cornerstones of Agile--translate directly into better AI collaboration. We'll explore real-world automation opportunities, how to teach AI to produce extensible, maintainable code, and why working with AI represents a new way of delivering value.

This is more than a technology update--it's a call to leaders and developers to improve at every level. If you want AI to make your team faster and better, Agile is the key--and the journey starts here.

David Scott Bernstein is the author of *Beyond Legacy Code* and co-author of *Prompt Engineering for Everyone*. With over four decades in software development, he has trained thousands of developers, leaders, and teams in Agile technical practices, clear thinking, and collaborative system design. His work now focuses on helping organizations partner with AI to improve quality, accelerate delivery, and foster innovation--while avoiding the pitfalls of unmanaged automation.

## Smile Sheet

### Testimonial

Years ago, I watched my dad shun CAD. Until he embraced it. When I asked him why he switched, he claimed "I always used the best tool available. Once CAD surpassed my drafting skills, I started using it".

I've observed a similar shift with David regarding what he once called "That blunt tool called English" because it lacked the specificity needed to define a system. He now uses English as the "source of truth" language for most of his interactions that build equivalent (or better) systems.

It's been wonderful to watch what has changed (David's stance toward English in system development) and what has not changed (David's embrace of the best tools available, adherence to agile engineering practices, and ability to change workflows when the state of the art advances).

### Insight

Deterministic systems should talk to deterministic systems. Ditto for non-deterministic systems.

This is an interesting evolution of David's preference for the precision of TDD many years ago. The tests are written in a deterministic, executable language, while specs use "That blunt tool called English". The interesting evolution is that he now uses "that blunt tool" in a different ecosystem and workflow to get results. Things have changed. But that rule still holds: deterministic/deterministic conversations and non-deterministic/non-deterministic conversations. This discontinuity underpins the phrase "discontinuous innovation".
